    National Retail Federation
    The Challenge

    It soon became clear that this was not going to be a simple redesign of a website, but so much more a tool to be used by an array of different parts of a large organization tasked with representing one of the largest industries in the United States; retail.

    With this project I wore more hats than just design. My hand touched the information architecture, content strategy, of course design, and taking over for a project manager in charge of all the existing content as well as new content, around a decades worth, making it to the new site.

    The Process

    I traveled to Washington D.C. with a few collegues to conduct stakeholder interviews with the client which kicked off our involvement with the project they needed accomplished.

    Content and Design Space

    Shortly thereafter, my digital trail of contribution started by undertaking an enormous content-audit which was more than just a long slog through a spreadsheet. It was vital in the effort to categorize and keep track of much existing content, as well as inform the new information architecture and design templates of a website which reached far beyond simple end-user content.


    Interaction Design

    Working closely with the team, I created these wireframes in our effort to come up with the templates that so much content and funtionality would be well fit. Starting with hand-drawn sketches of ideas for quick and light iteration, I then moved the ideas to this highly-annotated form to lead discussion with the client regarding our recommendation for how to present different content and functionality in different templates with one main breakpoint for responsive display.




    Content Management

    Over the last 2 months of the project, I took over for one of the project managers who had been in charge of all the new site's content on our end. However, a situation arose and I had to step in and do about 4 months worth of work, which was neglected, within the remaining 2 months. This included making extensive use of the content management system we had set up, keeping track of old content that was to be imported into the new site, working with Quality Assurance to make sure content being imported was up to standards, as well as heading ad-hoc teams to accomplish this importation of content. This also included flying back to the client's offices in Washington D.C. for a week, working with their teams and our development team, and training their team on the CMS, so future involvement from me going forward with new content would not be necessary.

    The Win

    More than just a new skin on a website was made. A more engaging and usable experience for the end user and a more usefull collaboration platform for internal teams found positive impact on everything from analytics on news published on the site to changing some of the internal collaboration flows of the organization for the better.

    Harmon's Grocery Redesign & custom CMS
    The Challenge

    A large grocery store chain in Utah needed a new website. However, it wasn't just a simple web page redesign. What they needed was a way to create and manage highly dynamic content, manage their ecommerce offerings and connect it to their point of sale system, manage fulfilment and in-store capabilities, schedule and manage sessions of in-store cooking classes they run, and so on. This warranted a necessity to design and implement a custom content management system (CMS) to accomplish all this, since most out of the box solutions either did not support requisite functionality or required too much configuration and shoehorning of functionality to accomplish these requirements. Additionally, great effort had to be done in order to get the product delivered within a very quick turnaround of three months. Remarkable for a site of this size and complexity.

    The Process

    We started with a content audit and current sitemap in order to communicate with the marketing team at Harmons which content was going to be imported to the new site, what content was to be removed, as well as in what new information heirarchy it was to be presented.

    This project saw heavy silmultaneous inter-departmental collaboration and iteration from the beginning until the end. Design, reviews, updates and iteration included all parts of the team. Project management, the client's marketing team, user experience and visual designers, and developers. In a sense, two projects were taking place in tandem. Both the website as seen by end-users, and the CMS which was a custom solution which especially needed to be intuative and simple to use while affording control of some rather complicated things. Things such as front-end content publishing with start and end dates, order fulfilment which had to account for current oven space and availability, the creation of promo codes and limits on their usage, sales reporting, etc. The wireframes shown here represent part of my contribution to these efforts.





    The Win

    The right team made all the difference here. Along with the selection of the right technologies, quick exploration and iteration of improvements in a very agile way, and keeping documentation light, we were able to deliver a solution for the client which not only fulfiled the scoped purpose, but gave them so much more. They saw noticable increases in interaction with many of their offerings to their customers, such as cooking class enrolment, online order fulfilment, and social media interaction and other user-generated content. The positive impact was especially seen shortly after launch, since the reason there was such a quick deadline for turnaround was that they aimed to have the site launched in time for the thanksgiving and christmas holidays in the United States.

    Adobe Financial Benefits Analytics Suite
    The Challenge

    A sales team at Adobe needed a tool to use in the field to present data visualizations to many high-level clients. Visualizations which needed to be not just aesthetically pleasing but informative and easily manipulated in context of a sales presentation. Moreover, it needed to work responsively across various device platforms, be accurate and engaging to the end audience.

    The Process

    After a couple days of presenting and sketching at Adobe, we started on a really tough, complicated web app with lots of numbers interacting with databases with a very dynamic display. Initial iteration with flow charts I created to get the complicated process and business requirements down before hours were spent on design and development

    While working closely and constantly iterating with developers, the team and I designed and implemented what was contained in many complicated spreadsheets into an intuitive flow with user-interactable controls to both visualize and manipulate the data. I created the wireframes below to guide that discussion during that process. These wireframes were not just a deliverable lobbed over the fence to inform design; however, they were a storytelling piece used to aid communication. They were changed and iterated upon every step of the way well through design and into development.



    The Win

    The win here was successfully giving Adobe sales teams a portable and visually engaging app with which to show dynamic numbers and time data to potential customers. Giving them the level of control and specificity over the numbers and data while having the output remain presentable and digestible to the presentee.

    The project was such a success, that after being presented internally at Adobe, we won a slough of subsequent projects implementing similar functionality for multiple additional departmens and needs at Adobe for this kind of thing.

    MusLab
    Simultaneous, Remote Music Collaboration
    The Challenge

    This project was completed as a quarter-long project for the capstone course of a HCI course series at UCSD. We were to work very collaboratively on a project which gave us valuable experience in that it required us to form a plan and perform interviews, experiments, deign according to the User Centered Design process all while being required to interact with people outside the university. It afforded real-world experience.

    The Process

    In less than ten weeks, our group had interviewed, observed, and usability tested over thirty musicians in various contexts, created affinity diagram trees and dynamic models, developed a series of wireframes, and ultimately presented our findings to the class and professorship under professional scrutiny.

    Methodology

    Methodology was key. The majority of the design cycle was spent on user research, either in the field or in artificial laboratories. At first, we interviewed a wide range of musicians, from a solo pianist who distributes her own podcast to a jamband with less than three shows under their belt to professors exploring remote music collaboration themselves and other professionals, we decided to narrow our scope of design to simply a jamming VoIP application. While we continued to collect data about the actual interaction between people in an orchestrated context, we began seeking data regarding such via computer-mediated interaction, latency included. To accomplish this, we set up musicians in different soundproofed rooms and connected them via Skype to play a piece together.

    After the data was collected, we would retreat to our brainstorm and begin diagramming what we saw. The team noted that latency was indeed the worst thing experienced by the musicians in tests, and the product was tweaked to accurately inform the user realistically how poor the latency is as well as override the issue with an artificial buffer.

    The Win

    In this project, I not only learned more how to create affinity note diagrams, prioritize product features while neglect others, and coordinate an evolving project with a hard deadline, but put it all in practice in real-world contexts. Ultimately, detailed wireframes with data-driven features were developed for this hypothetical program.

    PepsiCo - Corporate Site Redesign
    The Challenge
    Such a diverse corporation, made up of so much more than just the beverage, needed a content strategy and messaging presentation to rein it all in. So many parts and considerations to blend it all together under one umbrella while keeping the parts distinct was a challenge while integrating a site with a robust CMS and all the trappings of day to day corporate needs. Everything of course responsive and not just viewable but useful across devices, screen sizes and platforms.

    The Process
    Started with an immense content audit with which we based our content migration, omission, and identification of new content needs and opportunities for templating.

    This transitioned into the usual sketching, wireframing, click-throughs and client feedback. While it's easy to get into the rut of a corporate waterfall process, I kept it fresh and agile mainly through what a UX professional should be primarily concerned with anyway. Communication. While designing, I often did quick guerilla in-office paper tests with others on and off project, quick A/Bs online, and keeping developers and visual designers involved in my main steps well before they'd have otherwise been involved.



    The Win
    Our goals were reached, evident in the analytics metrics of overall hits as well as conversion having skyrocketed.

    Overall Goals

    Teaching some English and having a fun time is important, but what we are really here to do is to build relationships and provide a fun and engaging environment for some really sweet children who don't have it so easy at all. Many of them really look forward to this time all year, and the smiles you'll see on their faces when we arrive will make that apparent!

    The lesson times outlined are for what we prepare, yet the playtimes are often where we can shine. Much of the English learning really takes place during these periods when they are clearly eager to ask questions and try out their engish. Encourage them and be engaged with them. It'll make for an experience both you and they will never forget.
